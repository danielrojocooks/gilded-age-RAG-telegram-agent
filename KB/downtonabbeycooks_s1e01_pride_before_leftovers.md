## Episode Overview

HBO premiere of Julian Fellowes' The Gilded Age, set in 1882 New York City. Follows Marian Brook as she navigates society between old-money aristocrats and nouveau-riche industrialists (the Russell family).

## Food & Dining in Episode 1

### The Failed Soirée
Bertha Russell attempts to penetrate New York high society by hosting an elaborate open house. The kitchen prepared sophisticated French cuisine including:
- Lobster salad
- Croquembouche towers

When socialites declined to attend, chef Monsieur Baudin complained bitterly: "Cooking for paupers…that's not what I am used to" — the food was donated to the church instead.

George Russell sarcastically notes: "I don't know what the poor of New York will make of lobster salad."

### Culinary Philosophy of the Gilded Age Elite
Wealthy elites displayed social dominance through "rich and expensive ingredients, presented in the most fashionable French-named dishes." Extravagance defined Gilded Age dining, mirroring the Edwardian era.

### Class Divisions Through Food
When Bertha's lavish reception fails socially, the uneaten food donated to churches highlights how culinary extravagance couldn't overcome the deep divide between old and new money in 1880s society.

## American Culinary Development During the Gilded Age

Distinctly American dishes emerged from restaurant and hotel kitchens:
- Oysters Rockefeller
- Parker House Rolls
- Boston Cream Pie
- Palmer House Brownies

Working-class immigrants developed traditional foods based on heritage recipes and available ingredients. Culinary pioneers like Fannie Farmer published foundational cookbooks and opened cooking schools by 1902.
