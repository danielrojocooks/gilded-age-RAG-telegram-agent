Grover Cleveland, our twenty-second and twenty-fourth president was born in New Jersey, but raised in New York. He became a lawyer and practiced in Buffalo. He lived in a boarding house even though his salary was sufficient to allow him to live in a home, but he stayed in the boarding house because he was supporting his mother and younger sisters.

Because of his reputation, he was able to rise politically to the White House in three years. He won the Presidency with the combined support of Democrats and reform Republicans. He was the first Democrat elected after the Civil War.

The election campaign was vicious. The rumors were passed around by Cleveland’s opponent that he had terrible manners and Washington society visualized Jackson’s presidency when the carpets were smeared with cheese and the drapes were shredded.

It was true that he was not comfortable in the White House especially with the French food. He said, “I have to go to dinner, but I wish to eat a pickled herring, a Swiss cheese and a chop at Louis’ instead of the French stuff I shall find.” He was not comfortable with large formal affairs but he was dedicated to what the President and the White House should represent to the nation. To him this meant putting country above party at a time when this was not a popular course of action. He decided to keep the French chef from the former presidency, M. Fortin but he wrote to Governor Hill at Albany asking if he could have Eliza, the cook who had served him in the past. “She knows exactly what I like for breakfast: oatmeal, beefsteak, eggs or a chop with coffee.” Eliza moved into the White House and all was well.

He was single and 48 year old. Rose Cleveland, his younger sister was a teacher and a writer, and well-educated. She was also interested in social reform. She brought charm and culture to the White House as the official hostess.  Visitors found her to be an excellent conversationalist and always displayed gentility. A paragraph from the First Ladies Cookbook told of the newspaper correspondent, Frank Carpenter who said, “At state dinners, she sits in the great dining room like a queen, but she has to smile when a backwoods Congressman, dressed in his long frock coat, eats with his knife or drinks the thin lemon water out of the finger bowls.” She continued to lecture and was the author of several books. One of them received such good reviews there were twelve editions published. She admitted about herself, that she relieved the tedious hand shaking for hours in receiving lines by conjugating Greek verbs.

Politically, Cleveland was the leader of the Bourbon Democrats who were pro-business, who opposed high tariffs, Free Silver inflation, subsidies to business, farmers or veterans. For these reasons he became an example of conservatism. As with many other presidents, there were some good bills passed and some that were terrible and hurt many people struggling in life. For example, when Congress pressured by the Army to pass a bill granting pensions for disabilities not caused by military service, Cleveland vetoed it. He also vetoed a bill to grant $10,000 to distribute seed grain amoung drought-stricken farmers in Texas. He wrote, “Federal aid in such cases encourages the expectation of paternal care on the part of the government and weakens the sturdiness of our national charter.” Historians on many websites report that he was honest, had integrity and commitment to liberalism. Let’s check the honesty. During the Civil War, Congress passed the Conscription Act of 1863, which was the equivelant of the Draft, but there was an option. A man could hire a substitute to serve for him which Cleveland did. He paid George Benninsky, a 32 year old Polish immigrant $150.00 to serve in his place. I wonder if he lived through the war?

For many years, the presidential election had been decided based on the issue of slavery, but by the end of the Civil War, the public began thinking about the economy. Cleveland’s opponents including Benjamin Harrison and the Republican Party were proposing an inflationist policy of silver coinage, which they hoped would stimulate the economy. The other subject on the public’s mind was high protective tariffs. It was the Sherman Silver Act which forced the government to buy more silver each month which could be purchased by consumers with notes. These notes could also be used to buy gold which consumers did, depleting the nation’s gold reserves. Fortunately Cleveland had been opposed to this measure prior to the end of his first term as President, but it didn’t stop the pro-gold, high tariff Republicans. This argument continued all during the remainder of the 19th Century, but politics could not be blamed for this issue, as both parties were divided. Eventually this ruined the Democratic Party. “The parties were realigned ending the Third Party System, starting up the Fourth Party System and the Progressive Era.”

On the positive side, he helped usher in the beginning of Federal regulation. He fought against what he saw as private abuses of federal money. He brought about the Presidential Succession Act of 1886 which said if the President and Vice President were eleminated, the succession was to go through the cabinet in chronological order. In 1887, he passed the Interstate Commerce Act, creating the Interstate Commerce Commission which was to regulate interstate railroad rates. This was the first Federal regulatory agency. The railroads were furious with him because he ordered an investigation of western lands they held by government grant. He forced them to return 81,000,000 acres and signed the Interstate Commerce Act, the first law attempting Federal regulation of the railroads.  Later that year, he asked Congress to reduce high protective tariffs. He was told he had given the Republicians an effective issue for the next campaign. He said, “What is the use of being elected or re-elected unless you stand for sonething?”

Socially, Washington society didn’t expect much from President Cleveland because he cared little for receptions and entertained only when it was absolutely necessary, but he did shake a lot of hands. Then, everything changed. It seems President Cleveland had a secret. Miss. Francis Folsom had been his legal ward since her father’s death when she was only eleven years old. Her father had been Cleveland’s law partner. He had courted her with the permission of her mother after Francis became of age. After “Frank” as she was nicknamed by him graduated from college, she and her mother visited the White House and Francis’s affection grew and turned into love. They became engaged, secretly. She was only 22 years old and beautiful. They married in the White House in July of 1886, This was the first wedding of a president in the White House. They decided to hold the private wedding there for security reasons. The President wanted to keep everything simple and only his Cabinet and a few close personal friends were invited. They married in the Blue Room which had been decorated from floor to ceiling with flowers.

After their brief honeymoon, the journalists would not leave them alone so they purchased a home in the suburbs of Washington so they could lead a normal life except during the social season.

Rose Cleveland gladly gave up her duties to pursue her own career in education, The new Mrs. Cleveland became very popular as she was charming and loved to entertain. She held two receptions a week, one on Saturday afternoons, when women with jobs were free to come. Thank you, Francis\!

Her official social program opened on New Year’s Day and was described in *Carp’s Washington as “gorgeous, gay and giddy.* The mansion never looked cleaner or grander, and since it was open to any who might wish to come, the crowd had never before been larger. The dear people come by the thousands. Of all classes, ages and sizes, of all colors, sexes and conditions, they formed long lines reaching from the White House door around the paved walk all the way to the War Department.”

At levees and luncheons, President Cleveland would give each of his dinner guests a souvenir. Each lady received a ribbon about three feet long and three inches wide, on one end of which was stamped a picture of the White House and on the other end the national colors, with the coat of arms of the United States in gold. To this was added the name of the guest and the date of the dinner in gold letters. Can you see all the ladies pressing this treasure in their diaries?

The attendance at receptions during the last social season averaged 9,000 people.

The President was defeated in 1888 and the Clevelands moved to New York City where Ruth was born. He was re-elected four year later in 1892\. He was the only President to leave the White House and return for a second term four years later. The public loved the fact that there were babies in the White House. Esther was born in 1893 and Marion in 1895\. Mrs. Cleveland became one of the most popoular hostesses to serve in the White House.

But, Cleveland faced one of the worst depressions the nation had ever experienced and the panic of 1893\. One out of ten banks closed. He dealt with the Treasury crisis instead of the business failures, farm mortgage foreclosures and unemployment. He obtained repeal of the Sherman Silver Act, maintaining the Treasury’s gold reserve, but that was one of the issues of lesser importance. He sent Federal troops to some areas where striking railroad workers were, in order to keep the peace. “This blunt treatment of the strikers stirred the pride of many Americans.” He blamed the economic crisis on the Sherman Silver Act and the Republicans. To the public during his second term, he seemed overwhelmed by the nation’s economic disasters. The depression lasted his entire term of office weakening the Democratic Party. His party deserted him and nominated William Jennings Bryan but the Republican candidate, William McKinley was elected.

After leaving the White House, Cleveland retired to his home in Princeton, New Jersey. Francis bore two sons there and was at her husband’s side when he died at their home, “Westland” in 1908\.

President Cleveland’s favorite foods were corned beef and cabbage, Francis’s brown bread, Snickerdoodles and White cake with Chocolate frosting.

                         	***Roast Beef, French Style with Au Jus***

***Although Cleveland preferred the cooking from home, their French chef continued to cook in the French style for parties and banquets. This is an easy roast beef recipe with great au jus.***

***Preheat oven to 425 degrees***

***1- 3 to 3 1/2 pound eye of round roast, sirloin or rump roast. To cook a roast like this to a well-done temperature will ruin it. It will be very tough.  If you like your beef  roast well-done, I suggest you  cook a pot roast.***

***First, make 6-8 slits at various place in the roast just large enough to push a sliver of garlic in each slit. Rub entire roast with a combination of  two tablespoons soft butter, 1/8-1/4 teaspoon oregano and 1/4 teaspoon marjorum.  Sprinkle with salt and pepper and place in a roasting pan that is no larger than 9″ x 13″. If you use a large roasting pan, the juices will dry up during the roasting and there will be no a jus.***

***Place meat in oven and roast 20 minutes. During this time, turn the roast over once. After 20 minutes turn down temperature to 325 degrees and continue to roast about one hour and fifteen minutes to one hour and thirty minutes depending on the degree of doneness you prefer. This type of roast is meant to be sliced thinly for tenderness. Therfore, it needs to roast until it reaches 135-140 degrees. I prefer to go by the time rather than a thermometer due to altitude adjustments.***

                                                       	***Sauce***

***In a small bowl, combine 1 can Consomme, four tablespoons ketchip, one teaspoon brown mustard (Gulden’s), one teaspoon Worcestershire, one clove garlic, minced, and 1/3 cup water.***

***Afer the roast has cooked for 20 minutes at the lower temperature, pour the sauce over the roast. I usually add the tops and leaves of a few stalks of celery, an onion and a carrot, chopped. The vegetables add to the flavors. The roast is lean so there is no need to worry about grease. Plate the slices of roast and spoon some of the sauce over each slice. Serve with roasted vegetables and potatoes.***
