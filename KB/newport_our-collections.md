Our Collections

The Preservation Society of Newport County has in its care 11 historic houses (1748-1902), which together with their gardens, interiors, furnishings and archives constitute a unique cultural museum spanning the Colonial period through the Gilded Age.
The permanent collection is robust and diverse in content, including paintings, sculpture, furniture, ceramics and glass, gilded objects, silver and base metal objects, books, prints, lithographs, engravings, photographs and textiles, including tapestries, rugs, wall coverings, window hangings and period dress. The Preservation Society actively seeks out acquisitions for the permanent collection that are original to these historic spaces or reflect important themes each site, family or historical narrative shares through interpretative experiences.
The archival collection encompasses the organizational history of the Preservation Society from its founding in 1945 as well as materials related to the history of the society’s properties, people associated with them, and the history of Newport.

Collections Management and Standards
Collections management is, like building preservation, a core mission activity of The Preservation Society of Newport County and carries with it particular fiduciary responsibilities and obligations. As an accredited museum, the Preservation Society abides by governance standards and professional practices established by the American Alliance of Museums (AAM) and the Society of American Archivists (SAA).
We offer a principled approach to collections acquisition, accessioning, deaccessioning, access, loans, inventory, conservation, preservation and collections documentation. Guided by a Collections Management Policy, the Preservation Society’s collections management philosophy centers on standards of professional ethics over any intervention affecting the physical fabric of its holdings, discernment in acquiring the most representative and/or historically accurate objects and materials feasible, and concern for the delicate balance of the Preservation Society properties’ contextual settings. Collection objects that have been accessioned into the Preservation Society’s permanent collection become an integral part of the Preservation Society and are therefore placed under the protective mantle of the public trust. Accessioned objects will be exhibited, stored and cared for to the highest standard.
Managing the Preservation Society’s collections requires control over both the physical aspects of materials and the intellectual content they represent.
Preservation Society Deaccession Initiative: FAQs and Answers
The Preservation Society of Newport County has been undertaking a multi-year Deaccession Initiative since 2023. The deaccession process is a standard aspect of museum collections management which removes an object from our permanent collection because it cannot be conserved, cared for, or does not support our educational mission. Deaccessioned objects may not align with our interpretive goals, which prioritize objects with a direct historical association to one of our properties that is documented by archival or photographic evidence. For example, the purchase of a statue sculpted by Pio Fedi (Italian, 1816-1892) original to The Elms recreates the interior decoration and art collection present in the house during the Berwind family occupation. We also rely on objects with a secondary historical connection that are directly associated with the occupants of Preservation Society properties. For example, the bronze relief sculptures
Blinded
and
America at War
by Gertrude Vanderbilt Whitney (American, 1875-1942) provide contextual information about the artistic practices of Gertrude, who resided at The Breakers.
Deaccessioning is intended to strengthen our collection and is carried out with the highest accountability. We have exhaustively researched each object that has been deaccessioned. Once formally deaccessioned, the disposal of an object is determined on a case-by-case basis through careful research and deliberation. The Preservation Society plans to undertake direct transfer to another museum or non-profit institution and sale at public auction as our preferred methods of object removal.

What is deaccessioning?

The deaccession process is a standard aspect of museum collections management.
Deaccession is a formal, legal process in which an object is removed permanently from the museum’s permanent collection because it cannot be conserved, cared for, or fails to support the interpretive goals and educational purpose of the organization.
We are deaccessioning objects that do not align with our interpretive goals, which prioritizes objects with a direct historical association to one of our properties, as documented by archival or photographic documentation, and can be displayed to support the interpretation of the property (example: purchase of the statue of Michelangelo sculpted by Pio Fedi from The Elms). Q:
Why are we deaccessioning these donations?
−
+
To be good stewards of our collection and our houses, we must periodically review our permanent collection objects to determine whether they are in accordance with our collecting policy and mission. Our team works diligently to research our permanent collection objects as we continue to interpret our spaces and historical narratives.
An object may be deaccessioned if it is a duplicate of an existing object in the collection, inauthentic, in poor physical condition/unable to be cared for, or no longer serves the stated mission of the institution.
CURRENT MISSION

Q:
Can it be returned to the donor?
−
+
Returning an object would create complications for the donor if they took a tax deduction when the gift was made. Additionally, recalling a donation removes the object from the public trust and returns it to private hands without any compensation for the public.
Private sale or return of a donation is discouraged because it presents a conflict of interest and lacks the transparency of a public auction.
In donating these objects to the Preservation Society, a non-profit public museum, they were placed in public trust and therefore must remain in public trust (cannot be removed to private ownership without auction).
We have confirmed that the title to the object has been fully and freely transferred upon donation; the donor or next of kin has no legal authority to approve or object to the deaccessioning proposal.
Q:
What is going to happen to the object(s) once deaccessioned?
−
+
The method of disposal is determined on a case-by-case basis as a result of careful research and deliberation.
To keep the object within public trust, it will be disposed of through direct sale, exchange or donation to another museum or non-profit institution,
OR
the object will be sold at public auction.
The American Alliance of Museums maintains very strict limitations on the use of funds gained from deaccession, as a safeguard to prevent the sale of collections to raise funds to address short-term financial needs or projects. All proceeds resulting from the disposal of an object must be deposited in The Preservation Society of Newport County Collections Fund, which supports the direct care of our collections and finances the acquisition of new items.
