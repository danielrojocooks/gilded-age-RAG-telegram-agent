Early life

Bertha comes from humble beginnings, and her mother died in poverty. Her recent ancestors are said to have been potato farmers from Kerry,
and she has been called a "potato digger's daughter" by
Anne Morris
.
She has at least one sister,
Monica
.
She married
George Russell
, who became an incredibly wealthy railroad tycoon. She had two children,
Larry
and
Gladys
, and always wanted to break into respectable society. After living on 30th Street in Manhattan, she chose to move the family to 61st Street so that they could be accepted by the city's old money.
Season 1
[
]
Desperate to be accepted into New York's society of wealthy families with long and old lineages, Bertha is a socialite who build her new status along with her husband. She leverages her husband's wealth and flaunts whatever she can to impress, though it is her lowly background that hinders her when the old families and socialites look down upon new money like hers. She prevents her daughter from debuting into society until she is sure that the Russell family ballroom can be filled with individuals of note, but her constant attempts to integrate herself into these social circles and to raise her children to advantageous positions are seen as too presumptuous and unremarkable. After a failed ball and a snubbed offer to host a charity bazaar, Bertha vows revenge and, with her husband's help, outbuys an bazaar as a show of wealth and control over polite society, all the while managing to rein in her daughter Gladys with her many suitors. Through sly maneuvers, she manages to secure the invitations to her daughter's debutante ball, as well as the attendance of Gladys' friends and their parents, and the Russel family's debut into society has finally begun.
Personality
[
]
Bertha is a savvy, cunning and ambitious woman, eager to be fully accepted by New York's old money. She can be cold and mean when feels is necessary, though she can show a soft and kind side, specially with her husband whom she dearly loves, and is often seen assisting him with his schemes in his business, they help each other and are a solid team that never stop until they get what they want.

Larry Russell
[
]
Bertha has a better relationship with Larry than with Gladys. However, she is also not above meddling in his life if it makes gain some benefit. Nevertheless, Larry is constantly aware of Bertha's intentions and sometimes is able to outmaneuver her in order to help Gladys.
After Bertha arranges Gladys' marriage with Hector, Larry is visibly upset with his mother, and is against the idea of forcing Gladys into a marriage with a man she doesn't like. Their relationship becomes more strained when Marian calls off her engagement with Larry, who accuses Bertha of having a hand in all of it and leaves their house moving to a club.
After George survives his murder attempt, Bertha is visibly regretful of how her son has distanced from her, and whishes that he and Marian could reconcile. The morning after the ball of Newport, Larry remains firm in his decision of staying at the club, indicating that he still hasn't forgiven his mother, which affects Bertha.
Gladys Russell
[

She and Gladys have always had a complicated relationship. While Bertha loves Gladys, she tries to constantly control her life, prepare her for society and make sure she can gain an opportunity of having an exceptional life, more than she ever had before marrying George. For this reason, she is constantly seen dismissing every suitor Gladys has.

Trivia
[
]
Bertha Russell's character is inspired by Alva Vanderbilt.
Bertha's wardrobe is intentionally designed as a little forward of her time, taking inspiration from a wider scale of years between 1880-1910, and sometimes quite stylized. She wears a lot of steel tones in homage to the booming industrialism.
